Higher education institutions operate under financial, regulatory, and operational constraints that have no parallel in the corporate world. A mid-size university manages a student lifecycle spanning recruitment, admissions, enrollment, financial aid packaging, academic advising, degree audit, graduation, and alumni engagement — each stage governed by federal regulations, accreditation standards, and institutional policies that change on overlapping cycles. Revenue arrives through tuition, state appropriations, federal grants, endowment income, auxiliary services, and donor gifts, each with its own accounting treatment and compliance requirements.

The ERP system that manages all of this must handle fund accounting with GASB or FASB compliance, Title IV financial aid processing, and integration with student information systems that track every credit hour from orientation to commencement. This guide evaluates the seven ERP platforms most commonly deployed in higher education in 2026.

## Why Higher Education Needs Specialized ERP

Generic ERP platforms designed for manufacturing or retail fail in higher education for structural reasons that no amount of configuration can resolve.

**Fund accounting is not optional.** Universities do not operate on a single general ledger the way corporations do. A public university tracks hundreds of funds — each with its own revenue sources, spending restrictions, and reporting requirements. Federal research grants require effort reporting following OMB Uniform Guidance. Endowment funds carry donor-imposed restrictions spanning decades. An ERP without native fund accounting forces institutions into parallel spreadsheet systems that inevitably produce audit findings.

**Financial aid is a federal compliance operation.** Title IV processing is governed by the Department of Education's Federal Student Aid handbook, which exceeds 1,500 pages and changes annually. Satisfactory academic progress monitoring, return of Title IV funds calculations, and Pell Grant lifetime eligibility tracking must all function within regulatory tolerances that carry institutional eligibility risk. An ERP that treats financial aid as a bolt-on creates compliance exposure that can threaten an institution's ability to participate in federal student aid programs.

**The student lifecycle is unlike any commercial customer journey.** A prospective student moves through inquiry, application, admission, enrollment, registration, advising, degree audit, graduation, and alumni engagement over four to six years. Each transition generates financial transactions, compliance checkpoints, and reporting obligations. CRM-to-SIS-to-ERP integration must be seamless, or students fall through cracks that cost both revenue and reputation.

**HR complexity is structurally different.** A university employs tenured faculty on nine-month or twelve-month contracts, adjunct instructors paid per credit hour, classified staff on state pay scales, graduate assistants with tuition remission, and student workers limited to twenty hours weekly. Payroll must handle biweekly and monthly cycles simultaneously, often for the same employee holding multiple appointments. Generic HR modules cannot model this without significant customization.

## Top ERP Systems for Higher Education

### Workday Student

Workday entered higher education with its Student module in 2019 and has gained traction at large research universities that view cloud-native architecture as a strategic requirement.

**Student lifecycle and financial aid.** Workday Student provides a unified data model from recruitment through alumni engagement. Curriculum management, degree audit, registration, and transcript generation are native. The financial aid module covers Title IV processing, institutional aid packaging, and satisfactory academic progress monitoring, with 2025-26 FAFSA Simplification changes supported in current releases.

**Finance and HR.** Workday Financial Management supports GASB and FASB reporting with worktags replacing traditional chartfield structures. Grant management includes effort reporting and OMB Uniform Guidance compliance. The HCM module is where Workday's advantage is most pronounced — faculty contracts, adjunct pay, graduate assistant appointments, and student employment are handled within a unified framework with concurrent biweekly and monthly payroll.

**Weaknesses.** Implementations regularly exceed three years. Total cost of ownership is the highest in the market. The constrained customization model frustrates institutions with deeply embedded unique processes.

**Best for:** R1 research universities with the budget and patience for a multi-year transformation, especially those already running Workday HCM and Financials.

### Ellucian Banner

Banner is the most widely deployed ERP in American higher education, running at over 1,400 institutions. Its longevity creates both its greatest strength — decades of regulatory compliance built in — and its greatest challenge, as institutions weigh modernization against the risk of replacing a system that works.

**Student lifecycle and financial aid.** Banner Student is the benchmark for student information systems. Admissions, registration, academic history, degree audit via DegreeWorks, and graduation processing are deeply integrated. The system handles cross-registration, consortium agreements, and competency-based education. Banner Financial Aid is the gold standard for Title IV compliance — Pell Grant processing, Direct Loan origination, campus-based aid allocation, and return of Title IV calculations are all native, with a regulatory update cycle tightly aligned to Department of Education guidance.

**Finance and HR.** Banner Finance supports fund accounting with GASB and FASB compliance, budget management, and grant accounting. Banner HR covers position management, benefits, and payroll but is widely regarded as the suite's weakest component, leading many institutions to pair Banner Student and Finance with Workday HCM.

**Weaknesses.** The user interface still reflects 1990s architecture. Performance tuning requires dedicated DBA expertise. Decades of customization create upgrade barriers that trap institutions on older versions.

**Best for:** Mid-to-large universities needing proven Title IV compliance and deep student lifecycle functionality.

### Ellucian Colleague

Colleague serves small-to-mid-size private colleges and community colleges needing comprehensive functionality without Banner's infrastructure overhead or Workday's cost.

**Strengths.** Colleague provides admissions, registration, academic records, degree audit, and Title IV financial aid in a tightly integrated package for institutions under 15,000 enrollment. Fund accounting supports standard higher education chart of accounts structures. Retention alerts and predictive analytics are available through Ellucian's CRM Advise integration.

**Weaknesses.** Technical architecture limits scalability. Ad hoc reporting remains difficult. The SaaS modernization path is earlier in its maturity curve than Banner SaaS.

**Best for:** Community colleges and small private institutions with enrollment under 10,000.

### Oracle Cloud ERP with PeopleSoft Campus Solutions

PeopleSoft Campus Solutions remains the student information system of choice at many of the world's largest research universities. Oracle's current strategy encourages institutions to adopt Oracle Cloud ERP for financials and HCM while maintaining PeopleSoft for student records.

**Student lifecycle and financial aid.** PeopleSoft Campus Solutions is the most functionally deep SIS available for large, complex universities, handling multi-campus, multi-career, and multi-program enrollment scenarios that simpler platforms cannot model. PeopleSoft Financial Aid is Banner's only true peer in Title IV compliance depth, with mature packaging algorithms and satisfactory academic progress rules tested across hundreds of institutions.

**Finance and HR.** Oracle Cloud Financials delivers advanced fund accounting with GASB and FASB compliance, grant management aligned with OMB Uniform Guidance, and multi-entity consolidation. Oracle Cloud HCM provides strong workforce management, and the gap with Workday HCM for higher education has narrowed significantly.

**Weaknesses.** The PeopleSoft-to-Oracle-Cloud migration is smoother in marketing materials than in practice. PeopleSoft Campus Solutions has no Oracle Cloud replacement on the roadmap, creating architectural tension. PeopleTools expertise is increasingly difficult to hire.

**Best for:** Large research universities and multi-campus state systems with complex enrollment models, especially those already running PeopleSoft.

### Banner vs. PeopleSoft: The Decision That Defines a Decade

This remains the most consequential ERP decision most universities face.

**Functional depth.** PeopleSoft offers more configuration options for complex enrollment — multi-career students, cross-institutional enrollment, competency-based education. Banner provides streamlined workflows for standard models. Both are comparable in Title IV compliance.

**Total cost of ownership.** Banner's cost is generally 20-35% lower over a ten-year horizon, driven by lower infrastructure requirements and a larger consultant pool. PeopleSoft's premium reflects greater configurability and Oracle database licensing.

**Modernization path.** Ellucian is migrating Banner to SaaS with modern APIs. Oracle directs PeopleSoft institutions toward Cloud ERP for back-office functions while maintaining Campus Solutions indefinitely. Banner's migration involves one vendor's roadmap; PeopleSoft's involves navigating between two Oracle product lines.

**Verdict.** Institutions under 20,000 enrollment with standard models should prefer Banner. Institutions over 30,000 with complex multi-campus structures should prefer PeopleSoft. Those in between should evaluate based on existing vendor relationships and regional talent availability.

### SAP S/4HANA for Higher Education

SAP's higher education presence is strongest among large global universities, particularly in Europe, the Middle East, and Asia-Pacific.

**Strengths.** SAP delivers the most sophisticated financial management platform available — multi-currency, multi-GAAP, and multi-entity capabilities serve universities operating across regulatory jurisdictions. Grant management and research administration are mature. HR handles complex international employment scenarios.

**Weaknesses.** SAP has no native student information system, requiring integration with Ellucian, Oracle, or a regional SIS. Implementations take three to five years. Cost puts SAP out of reach for institutions under 25,000 enrollment.

**Best for:** Large global universities, academic medical centers already running SAP, and institutions prioritizing financial sophistication over SIS integration simplicity.

### Unit4 ERPx

Unit4 has built a meaningful position in higher education in the UK, Canada, and Australia with a platform designed for people-intensive service organizations.

**Strengths.** Financial management and HR are well-suited to higher education without the implementation investment of SAP or Oracle. Fund accounting, grant management, and multi-entity consolidation are supported. Implementation timelines of 12 to 18 months are roughly half that of Workday or SAP.

**Weaknesses.** No native student information system. Limited North American presence constrains the available consultant pool. U.S. Title IV compliance requires third-party integration.

**Best for:** Mid-size institutions outside the United States needing strong financials and HR at lower cost.

### Anthology Student (formerly Campus Nexus)

Anthology offers a cloud-native student information and ERP platform aimed at small private colleges and career-focused institutions.

**Strengths.** A modern, unified platform with CRM, SIS, financial aid, and student financials. User experience is significantly more modern than Banner or Colleague. Implementation timelines of 12 to 18 months are realistic for institutions under 5,000 enrollment. Integration with Anthology's LMS (formerly Blackboard) creates a unified edtech stack.

**Weaknesses.** Lacks functional depth for complex enrollment scenarios. Financial management beyond student financials requires a separate ERP. Vendor stability remains a question after multiple mergers and private equity transactions.

**Best for:** Small private and career colleges under 5,000 enrollment prioritizing modern UX and rapid deployment.

## Implementation Realities

Higher education ERP implementations are governed by the academic calendar. A failed go-live during fall registration prevents students from enrolling, triggers financial aid disbursement delays, and generates press coverage that damages enrollment for years.

**Plan around the academic calendar.** Most institutions target July 1 to align with the fiscal year and allow stabilization before fall registration. The decision process must begin 24 to 36 months before that date.

**Budget for data migration.** Student records and financial history at a 50-year-old university contain decades of complexity. Data cleansing and migration typically consume 25 to 35 percent of implementation cost and are the primary source of timeline overruns.

**Invest in change management.** Faculty and long-tenured staff have deep familiarity with existing systems. Training, communication, and parallel processing periods are not optional. Institutions that underinvest report higher post-go-live costs and longer stabilization.

**Negotiate on Carnegie classification.** Vendors price contracts based on enrollment, Carnegie classification, and module scope. Community colleges and small private institutions should negotiate aggressively on per-student pricing and request peer references.

## Bottom Line

The right ERP depends on institutional size, existing technology investments, and realistic budget tolerance.

Institutions over 25,000 enrollment with complex research portfolios should evaluate **Workday Student** and **Oracle PeopleSoft Campus Solutions**, choosing between cloud-native architecture and functional depth in student records.

Mid-size institutions between 5,000 and 25,000 should start with **Ellucian Banner**, which offers the best balance of completeness, Title IV compliance, cost, and available talent.

Small colleges under 5,000 should evaluate **Ellucian Colleague** and **Anthology Student**, weighing proven track record against modern architecture.

Institutions outside the United States should add **Unit4** and **SAP** based on regional presence and multi-country requirements.

The ERP decision is a decade-long commitment. The selection process deserves the same rigor institutions apply to hiring a provost or approving a capital construction project — because the consequences of getting it wrong last just as long.
