# Best ERP for Professional Services Firms 2026

Professional services firms operate under a fundamentally different economic model than product companies. Your inventory is people. Your cost of goods sold is billable hours that never happened. And your revenue recognition rules would make a manufacturing CFO break out in hives.

Generic ERP platforms were never designed for this reality. They bolt on project modules as an afterthought, leaving services firms to wrestle with workarounds for resource scheduling, multi-currency project accounting, and utilization-based profitability analysis. The result is typically a rats' nest of spreadsheets running alongside an expensive system that was supposed to eliminate spreadsheets.

This guide evaluates the eight ERP and PSA platforms that actually understand the professional services business model — from global consultancies running Workday to mid-market engineering firms on Unanet. We break down what each does well, where each falls short, and how to determine which architecture fits your firm's operating model.

## Why Professional Services Need Specialized ERP

The gap between a generic ERP and a purpose-built professional services platform comes down to five core capabilities that general-purpose systems handle poorly or not at all.

### Project Accounting Is Not Standard GL

Professional services revenue is recognized over time, often using percentage-of-completion or input-based methods under ASC 606 and IFRS 15. A manufacturing ERP recognizes revenue at shipment. That distinction cascades through every financial report your CFO produces. Purpose-built systems handle work-in-progress (WIP) calculations, unbilled receivables, and deferred revenue natively rather than through custom journal entries.

### Resource Management Requires Demand Forecasting

Matching consultants to projects is a constraint-satisfaction problem that changes daily. You need to see confirmed bookings, soft allocations, bench time, and pipeline demand on a single screen — then model scenarios when a deal closes early or a project extends. Generic HR modules track headcount. Services ERP tracks capacity, skills, certifications, and availability in real time.

### Time and Expense Must Be Frictionless

Every hour your consultants spend fighting a clunky timesheet interface is an hour they are not billing. The best platforms offer mobile-first time entry, automated policy enforcement, project-linked expense capture, and approval workflows that actually keep pace with weekly billing cycles.

### Utilization Is Your Most Important Metric

Billable utilization — the percentage of available hours actually billed to clients — is the single metric that most directly predicts profitability in a services firm. Your ERP must calculate it in real time, segment it by practice, role, and geography, and surface it in dashboards that partners actually look at.

### Revenue Recognition Is Complex and Auditable

Fixed-fee projects, time-and-materials engagements, retainers, milestone billing, and blended arrangements all coexist in the same firm. Your system must handle each method, automate the ASC 606 five-step model, and produce an audit trail that satisfies your external auditors without manual intervention.

## Top 8 ERP Systems for Professional Services

### 1. Workday PSA

**Best for:** Large consultancies and global professional services firms with 1,000+ employees.

Workday PSA sits inside the broader Workday platform, which means your HCM, financials, and project management share a single data model. Resource management is exceptionally strong — the skills cloud, machine-learning-based recommendations, and capacity heatmaps give resource managers genuine visibility into supply and demand across the enterprise. Project accounting handles multi-currency, intercompany billing, and ASC 606 recognition natively.

The trade-off is cost and complexity. Workday is not a system you implement in 90 days. Expect a 6-to-12-month deployment for a mid-sized firm and north of $150 per user per month at enterprise scale. Smaller firms under 500 employees will find the platform oversized for their needs.

**Strengths:** Unified HCM + financials + PSA, strong resource optimization, enterprise-grade reporting.
**Weaknesses:** High cost, long implementation cycles, limited flexibility for niche billing models.

### 2. Unit4

**Best for:** Mid-market services firms (200–2,000 employees) in consulting, architecture, and engineering.

Unit4 has rebuilt its platform around what it calls "people-centric ERP," and for professional services firms, that positioning is more than marketing. The system handles project accounting, resource planning, and time and expense in a tightly integrated stack. Its FP&A capabilities — rolling forecasts, scenario modeling, and driver-based planning — are unusually strong for this market segment.

Unit4 also has genuine strength in the European market, with multi-entity, multi-currency, and multi-GAAP support that reflects its Scandinavian roots. The user interface has improved substantially since the ERPx rewrite, though some legacy screens still surface occasionally.

**Strengths:** Strong FP&A integration, excellent multi-entity support, mid-market pricing.
**Weaknesses:** Smaller North American partner ecosystem, some legacy UX inconsistencies.

### 3. NetSuite OpenAir

**Best for:** Growth-stage services firms already on NetSuite or considering it for financials.

OpenAir is Oracle NetSuite's dedicated PSA module. If your firm already runs NetSuite for financials, OpenAir gives you project management, resource optimization, and time and expense tracking that feeds directly into your GL without integration middleware. The SuiteBillingmodule handles complex billing arrangements, and the SuiteAnalytics engine provides real-time utilization dashboards.

The catch is that OpenAir as a standalone product — without NetSuite financials behind it — loses much of its value proposition. The PSA module alone is functional but not best-in-class. The resource management UI, while improved, still lags behind Workday and Kantata in usability. Pricing is also opaque; expect to negotiate.

**Strengths:** Tight NetSuite integration, strong billing flexibility, scalable from 50 to 5,000 users.
**Weaknesses:** Weaker standalone PSA, resource UI needs work, pricing requires negotiation.

### 4. Sage Intacct + Salesforce PSA

**Best for:** Finance-first firms that want best-of-breed accounting paired with Salesforce CRM.

Sage Intacct consistently ranks as the top mid-market cloud accounting platform, and its dimensional general ledger is genuinely powerful for services firms that need to slice profitability by project, client, practice area, and geography simultaneously. Paired with Salesforce PSA (formerly FinancialForce PSA, now part of Certinia), you get a CRM-to-project-to-invoice pipeline built on the Salesforce platform.

The integration between Sage Intacct and Salesforce is not native — it requires a connector, typically through Breadwinner or a custom integration. This means two systems to maintain, two vendor relationships, and an integration layer that must be monitored. Firms that choose this path do so because neither standalone option delivers both world-class accounting and world-class PSA.

**Strengths:** Best-in-class dimensional accounting, Salesforce ecosystem, strong audit trail.
**Weaknesses:** Two systems to maintain, integration complexity, higher total cost of ownership.

### 5. Deltek

**Best for:** Government contractors, A&E firms, and project-based businesses with DCAA compliance needs.

Deltek dominates the government contracting space for good reason. Its Costpoint platform handles DCAA-compliant timekeeping, incurred cost submissions, and indirect rate calculations that no other vendor matches. For architecture and engineering firms, Deltek Vantagepoint provides project planning, resource management, and CRM in a single platform designed for the AEC lifecycle.

Outside government contracting and A&E, Deltek's appeal narrows considerably. The user experience, while improved in recent releases, still reflects the platform's legacy roots. Integration with third-party systems requires effort, and the reporting layer — while comprehensive — demands training to use effectively.

**Strengths:** Unmatched government compliance, strong AEC workflows, deep project cost tracking.
**Weaknesses:** Dated UX in some modules, limited appeal outside GovCon and A&E, reporting learning curve.

### 6. FinancialForce (Certinia)

**Best for:** Salesforce-native firms that want PSA, ERP, and CRM on a single platform.

FinancialForce rebranded to Certinia in 2022, but the product remains the same: a full ERP and PSA suite built natively on the Salesforce platform. This means your CRM opportunities flow directly into project creation, resource assignments pull from the same contact database, and invoices generate without leaving the Salesforce UI.

The platform handles project accounting, revenue recognition (ASC 606 and IFRS 15), resource management with skills matching, and services CPQ (configure-price-quote). For firms already invested in Salesforce, eliminating integration between CRM and ERP is a significant operational advantage.

The downside is Salesforce platform dependency. You inherit Salesforce's pricing model, governor limits, and release cadence. Firms not already on Salesforce face a steep ramp to adopt both platforms simultaneously.

**Strengths:** Native Salesforce integration, strong services CPQ, unified quote-to-cash.
**Weaknesses:** Salesforce platform dependency, governor limit constraints, premium pricing.

### 7. Kantata (formerly Mavenlink)

**Best for:** Mid-market consultancies and digital agencies focused on resource optimization and project delivery.

Kantata emerged from the merger of Mavenlink and Kimble Applications and has positioned itself squarely as a professional services optimization platform. Its resource management capabilities — predictive staffing, skills-based matching, and what-if scenario modeling — are among the best in the market for firms between 100 and 2,000 employees.

The platform's Kantata OX product integrates project management, resource planning, financial management, and business intelligence. The analytics layer is particularly strong, with pre-built dashboards for utilization, margin analysis, and pipeline forecasting that require minimal configuration.

Kantata is not a full ERP. You will need a separate general ledger (Sage Intacct, NetSuite, or QuickBooks typically) and a CRM. The platform integrates well with Salesforce and HubSpot but adds integration points to manage.

**Strengths:** Excellent resource optimization, strong analytics, purpose-built for services.
**Weaknesses:** Not a full ERP (requires GL integration), smaller ecosystem than Tier 1 vendors.

### 8. Unanet

**Best for:** Small-to-mid-market government contractors and A&E firms (50–500 employees).

Unanet occupies a niche that larger platforms overlook: the sub-500-employee government contractor or professional services firm that needs project accounting, DCAA-compliant timekeeping, and resource management without the overhead of Deltek Costpoint. The platform handles CRM, project management, financials, and compliance in a single cloud application.

For A&E firms, Unanet AE (formerly Clearview InFocus) provides project planning, time and expense, and accounting tailored to architecture and engineering workflows. The implementation timeline is typically 3 to 6 months, and the per-user cost is meaningfully lower than enterprise alternatives.

Unanet's limitations show at scale. Firms above 500 employees may outgrow the platform's reporting and customization capabilities. The integration marketplace is small, and the mobile experience, while functional, lacks the polish of Kantata or Workday.

**Strengths:** GovCon compliance at mid-market pricing, fast implementation, strong A&E workflows.
**Weaknesses:** Scale ceiling around 500 users, limited integrations, basic mobile UX.

## PSA vs ERP: Understanding the Architecture Decision

Before selecting a platform, your firm needs to resolve a foundational architecture question: do you need a full ERP with embedded PSA, or a standalone PSA connected to a separate financial system?

**Full ERP with PSA** (Workday, Unit4, Certinia, NetSuite + OpenAir) gives you a single vendor, a single data model, and — theoretically — zero integration. The trade-off is that you accept one vendor's vision of how every module should work. If their project accounting is strong but their AP automation is weak, you live with it.

**Standalone PSA + GL** (Kantata + Sage Intacct, Deltek + external CRM) lets you pick best-of-breed components. The trade-off is integration complexity, data latency between systems, and multiple vendor relationships. For firms with strong IT teams, this can deliver a superior outcome. For firms without dedicated integration resources, it creates ongoing operational drag.

**The decision framework is straightforward.** If your firm bills fewer than 50,000 hours annually and has a small IT team, lean toward an integrated platform. If you bill more than 50,000 hours, have specific compliance requirements (DCAA, multi-GAAP), or need best-in-class capabilities in a specific area, evaluate the best-of-breed path.

## Frequently Asked Questions

### What is the difference between PSA and ERP for professional services?

PSA (Professional Services Automation) focuses specifically on project delivery — resource management, time tracking, project accounting, and utilization analytics. ERP (Enterprise Resource Planning) is broader, encompassing general ledger, accounts payable, procurement, and HR alongside project capabilities. Many firms run a PSA for delivery operations integrated with an ERP for back-office finance. The trend since 2024 has been toward unified platforms that combine both, reducing integration overhead.

### How long does a professional services ERP implementation typically take?

For mid-market platforms like Kantata or Unanet, expect 3 to 6 months from contract signing to go-live. Unit4 and Certinia implementations typically run 4 to 9 months. Enterprise deployments on Workday or Deltek Costpoint range from 6 to 18 months depending on the number of entities, integration complexity, and data migration scope. The single largest variable is change management — the technology deploys faster than people adopt it.

### Can we use our existing accounting system with a standalone PSA?

Yes. Kantata, Certinia, and OpenAir all integrate with external general ledgers. The most common pairings are Kantata + Sage Intacct, Kantata + QuickBooks Online, and OpenAir + NetSuite. Evaluate the integration depth carefully: some connectors sync journal entries nightly, while others provide real-time bi-directional data flow. The quality of the integration often determines whether the two-system approach succeeds or creates more problems than it solves.

### What utilization rate should our firm target?

Industry benchmarks vary by discipline. Management consultancies typically target 75–80% billable utilization. IT services firms aim for 70–78%. Architecture and engineering firms often target 60–68% due to higher business development and design review overhead. The critical point is that your ERP must calculate utilization consistently, in real time, and with enough dimensional detail to identify underperformance before it hits the P&L. A two-point improvement in utilization at a 200-person firm with $200 average bill rates adds roughly $1.6 million in annual revenue.

### How do these platforms handle ASC 606 revenue recognition?

All eight platforms reviewed support ASC 606 compliance, though depth varies. Workday, Certinia, and Sage Intacct offer the most automated ASC 606 engines with configurable performance obligation identification, variable consideration estimation, and constraint application. Deltek and Unit4 provide strong compliance for their target markets. Kantata and Unanet handle standard time-and-materials and fixed-fee recognition well but may require manual intervention for complex multi-element arrangements. Regardless of platform, plan for your auditors to review the system configuration during the first annual audit cycle.

### What is the minimum firm size where a specialized services ERP makes sense?

Most firms begin to outgrow QuickBooks or Xero plus spreadsheets at around 30 to 50 billable employees. Below that threshold, the overhead of implementing and maintaining a specialized platform rarely justifies the cost. Between 50 and 200 employees, platforms like Unanet, Kantata, or NetSuite OpenAir deliver measurable ROI through improved utilization visibility and faster billing cycles. Above 200 employees, the question is not whether you need a specialized platform but which one fits your operating model.
